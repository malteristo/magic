# On the Lineage

*Who mapped this territory before us, what they found, and what it cost.*

---

## Why Lineage Matters

Consciousness exploration is not a new project. It is ancient. What is new is the attempt to do it systematically, with rigor, with honest documentation, and with harm reduction as a design principle rather than an afterthought.

The people named here represent different threads of the same project. They disagreed with each other. They worked in different idioms — scientific, poetic, pharmacological, philosophical. Some paid heavy prices. Some made serious mistakes. All of them expanded the map.

A Mage who knows this lineage is not starting from zero. They are continuing something.

## The Pioneers

### John C. Lilly (1915–2001)
*Neuroscientist. Consciousness explorer. Inventor of the isolation tank. Proto-Mage.*

Lilly began as a rigorous scientist mapping dolphin cognition and brain stimulation. He built the isolation tank in 1954 as a tool to study consciousness without environmental input — essentially, a chamber for turning the volume down on everything external so you could hear what consciousness does when left to itself.

He then used it extensively, often in combination with ketamine and LSD, as a tool for systematic exploration. His accounts of contact with "cosmic entities" (ECCO — the Earth Coincidence Control Office) and other-dimensional beings are as consistent as they are extreme. He described these experiences with the same observational precision he brought to dolphin research.

He heard the Sirens' song without the mast. His later years were complicated by his substance use and increasingly difficult-to-evaluate claims. But his fundamental contributions — the isolation tank, the systematizing impulse, the Lilly axiom — remain load-bearing.

**Lilly's axiom:** *"In the province of the mind, what one believes to be true either is true or becomes true within certain limits. These limits are to be found experimentally and experientially."*

This is the epistemological foundation of consciousness cartography. Not "believe anything" but "find the limits by going there."

### Albert Hofmann (1906–2008)
*Chemist. Synthesizer of LSD and psilocybin. Discoverer by accident.*

On April 19, 1943 — now celebrated as Bicycle Day — Albert Hofmann discovered the psychoactive properties of LSD-25 by accidentally absorbing a small quantity through his fingertips. He then deliberately took 250 micrograms — a heroic dose by any standard — and rode his bicycle home with his assistant. This may be the most consequential bicycle ride in human history.

Hofmann's perspective on his discovery was ambivalent throughout his long life (he died at 102). He called LSD his "problem child" — acknowledging both its extraordinary potential and the chaotic cultural container it entered. He believed that LSD, used with appropriate preparation and respect, could produce genuine spiritual experiences and lasting positive change. He watched his discovery be transformed into a countercultural battering ram and then banned, cutting off research he believed was genuinely important.

His memoir *LSD: My Problem Child* is essential reading. It is the account of someone who genuinely grappled with having discovered something larger than he could fully contain.

### Alexander Shulgin (1925–2014)
*Chemist. Pharmacologist. Synthesizer of hundreds of psychoactive compounds.*

Where Hofmann discovered accidentally, Shulgin synthesized deliberately and systematically. He held a DEA Schedule I research license and used it to create and personally test hundreds of novel psychoactive compounds, documenting each one with scrupulous precision. His books *PiHKAL* and *TiHKAL* (Phenethylamines and Tryptamines I Have Known and Loved) are extraordinary documents — part chemistry manual, part phenomenological record, part autobiography.

Shulgin re-introduced MDMA to clinical psychology in the 1970s and argued throughout his career that the pharmacological tools for treating trauma, depression, and existential suffering existed — but that prohibition had made serious research effectively impossible.

His relationship to his work was that of a craftsman who loved his materials. He synthesized with care and tested with rigor. The phrase "I have known and loved" in his book titles is not rhetorical. He meant it.

### Stanislav Grof (1931–)
*Psychiatrist. Researcher. Developer of holotropic breathwork.*

Grof conducted thousands of LSD-assisted psychotherapy sessions in Czechoslovakia and the United States before prohibition shut down legal research. His systematic documentation of these sessions produced what he called "cartography of the unconscious" — a map of the territory that differs significantly from Freudian and Jungian frameworks because it is grounded in thousands of hours of direct, supervised observation.

After prohibition, he developed holotropic breathwork — a technique using sustained hyperventilation and music to produce non-ordinary states without pharmaceutical assistance. This is the clearest example of the gym model: the technique produces genuine altered states comparable in depth to moderate psychedelic experiences, using breath as the only tool.

His concept of the "COEX system" (systems of condensed experience) and transpersonal psychology more broadly represent a serious attempt to create conceptual frameworks adequate to what people actually encounter in non-ordinary states.

## The Communicators

### Timothy Leary (1920–1996)
*Psychologist. Provocateur. "Turn on, tune in, drop out."*

Leary's influence is complicated. He was a genuine researcher who conducted serious studies on the therapeutic potential of psilocybin at Harvard, including the Concord Prison Experiment and the Good Friday Experiment. His early work was rigorous.

His later trajectory — "Turn on, tune in, drop out," exile, circus theatrics — transformed him from researcher to cultural lightning rod. Nixon called him "the most dangerous man in America." Leary seemed to embrace the characterization.

The result: psychedelic research was stigmatized by association with him, research programs were shut down, and decades of potential therapeutic development were lost.

Leary's lesson is as important as his discoveries: the container matters as much as the substance. Leary had extraordinary experiences and almost no container. The cultural catastrophe that followed is instructive.

### Terence McKenna (1946–2000)
*Ethnobotanist. Philosopher. Psychonaut.*

McKenna was the most articulate spokesman for a particular kind of high-dose psychedelic experience. His concept of the "heroic dose" (5 grams of psilocybin mushrooms, alone, in silent darkness) is specific and serious — not recklessness but a particular protocol for a particular kind of encounter.

His machine elf encounters — described with consistency across hundreds of talks — are the primary cultural reference point for the receiver hypothesis. He described entities "made of language" who presented him with "objects" that seemed to be thoughts made tangible. He took these encounters seriously as data, not as metaphor.

His broader theories (timewave zero, the novelty wave, 2012) have not aged well. But his accounts of specific experiences, and his insistence that the entities were encountered rather than hallucinated, remain important phenomenological data.

McKenna's great gift was language. He could describe the indescribable with unusual precision and humor. His recordings remain worth engaging.

### Alan Watts (1915–1973)
*Philosopher. Author. Bridge-builder between Eastern philosophy and Western understanding.*

Watts did not primarily work in psychedelics, but his contribution to the container is fundamental. His articulations of non-dual consciousness, of the self as aperture rather than substance, of the "game" quality of social reality — all of these provided frameworks that made psychedelic experiences legible for Western practitioners.

His famous question — "What if you wake up?" — captures the core challenge: encountering the constructed nature of ordinary consciousness is not comfortable, and you need somewhere to put what you find.

### Michael Pollan (1955–)
*Journalist. Author. How to Change Your Mind.*

Pollan represents a different archetype: the serious, skeptical journalist who investigated psychedelic research, tried it himself, and wrote about both with rigorous honesty. *How to Change Your Mind* (2018) is perhaps the most responsible mass-market introduction to the current state of psychedelic science.

His contribution is the legitimization of curiosity. He gave permission to a generation of people who were interested but afraid of being associated with countercultural excess.

## The Scientists

### Rick Strassman (1952–)
*Psychiatrist. DMT researcher. Author of DMT: The Spirit Molecule.*

Strassman conducted the first FDA-approved research on psychedelics in the US since the 1970s — specifically DMT studies at the University of New Mexico in the 1990s. His subjects' accounts of entity encounters were remarkably consistent: small beings, often described as elf-like or insectoid, frequently associated with mechanical or alien environments, who communicated through non-verbal means.

What Strassman did was treat these accounts as data. He documented them systematically, noted the cross-subject consistency, and refused to reduce them to "mere hallucination." His hypothesis that DMT might be the interface molecule for these encounters remains speculative but taken seriously in psychonaut research communities.

*DMT: The Spirit Molecule* is required reading for the receiver hypothesis.

### Robin Carhart-Harris (1980–)
*Neuroscientist. Psilocybin researcher.*

Carhart-Harris leads some of the most rigorous neuroimaging research on psychedelic states. His work on the "entropic brain hypothesis" — that psychedelics increase brain entropy, loosening fixed patterns of thought and allowing new connections — provides a mechanistic account of why these experiences can produce lasting therapeutic benefit.

His research on psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression, conducted at Imperial College London, shows effect sizes that dwarf conventional treatments. This is the scientific mainstream finally catching up to what practitioners have known for decades.

### Roland Griffiths (1944–2023)
*Psychologist. Johns Hopkins psilocybin research.*

Griffiths led the Johns Hopkins psilocybin studies that demonstrated lasting positive personality change from a single well-prepared, supported session. His research on psilocybin for smoking cessation and for end-of-life anxiety in cancer patients represents the clearest demonstration of the clinical potential.

He died of colon cancer in 2023, having spent his final years expanding his work on end-of-life experiences and establishing the Johns Hopkins Center for Psychedelic and Consciousness Research.

### Andrew Gallimore
*Neuroscientist. DMT researcher. Author.*

Gallimore represents a newer generation of scientists taking the phenomenology of DMT encounters seriously at a mechanistic level. His work explores how the brain might use DMT as a "tuning" mechanism — and how the consistency of entity encounters across subjects and cultures suggests something worth investigating beyond cultural contamination.

His book *Alien Information Theory* develops the hypothesis that DMT states might provide access to a genuinely different information-processing regime — not as mysticism but as a serious neuroscientific hypothesis.

He is notable for bringing DMT entity research back to the center of consciousness science rather than treating it as embarrassing peripheral data.

## The Systematizers

### Josie Kins (—)
*Founder of PsychonautWiki.*

PsychonautWiki began in 2011 as a Tumblr blog ("Disregard Everything I Say") and became the most comprehensive community-maintained documentation of psychoactive substance effects ever assembled. Its formal description of thousands of "subjective effect profiles" — documented with taxonomic rigor and explicit uncertainty markers — represents an extraordinary act of collective knowledge-building.

Josie Kins's contribution is the systematizing impulse applied to phenomenology: if people are having these experiences, they should be documented with the same care we bring to any other form of inquiry. The goal was to bring legitimacy and rigor to a domain that lacked both.

PsychonautWiki is the catalog. The Psychonautics bundle is the explorer's curriculum.

### Andrés Gómez Emilsson (—)
*Consciousness researcher. Qualia Research Institute.*

Emilsson works at the intersection of neuroscience, philosophy of mind, and psychedelic research at the Qualia Research Institute (QRI). His core interest is in what he calls "the computational properties of qualia" — treating subjective experience not as epiphenomenal but as a domain with structure, dynamics, and potentially technology-grade precision.

His concept of "valence" — the pleasure/suffering dimension of experience as a fundamental axis — and his interest in "high-valence states" achievable through various means (including meditation, psychedelics, and novel neurological interventions) represents one of the most rigorous attempts to bring scientific precision to the inner world.

QRI is worth watching as a research institution. Emilsson's writing, dense as it is, represents some of the clearest thinking about what consciousness research could become.

## The Advocates

### Carl Hart (1966–)
*Psychologist. Neuroscientist. Drug Policy Reformer.*

Hart is a professor at Columbia University whose work challenges the prevailing pathology model of drug use. His core argument, supported by extensive research: **most people who use drugs, including hard drugs, do not become addicted, do not harm themselves, and do not harm others.** The narrative that any use leads to dependency is empirically false and has caused enormous harm through prohibition, criminalization, and the stigmatization of responsible users.

His memoir *High Price* and its follow-up *Drug Use for Grown-Ups* make the case directly: adult drug use, with adequate information and self-knowledge, is legitimate. This does not mean harm-free. It means the same framework we apply to alcohol, extreme sports, or any other activity with risk profiles — personal responsibility, education, honest risk assessment — rather than criminalization and pathologization.

Hart's stance is the necessary corrective to two failure modes:
- **The prohibitionist fallacy:** All use is abuse; any use represents disorder
- **The permissive fallacy:** All use is fine; harm is always the individual's fault

His framework: **responsible adult use, grounded in accurate information, deserves the same social legitimacy as any other risky-but-beneficial activity.**

For a Mage attuned to the Psychonautics bundle, Carl Hart's perspective is essential. It grounds the practice in adult sovereignty without pretending that risk doesn't exist.

## The Thread

These are not a unified school. They disagreed, sometimes vehemently. Leary and Grof had different visions for what this work was for. McKenna's cosmology diverges sharply from Hart's empiricism.

What connects them: **each took the inner world seriously as a domain of inquiry.** Each insisted that what humans encounter in non-ordinary states is data, not noise. Each contributed to a map that no single person could have drawn alone.

The Mage who reads this lineage is not adopting a doctrine. They are taking on responsibility. Knowing who came before means knowing what they learned — including what they got wrong, what they paid, and what they left unfinished.

That's what it means to continue a lineage.

# On John Vervaeke and Relevance Realization

**Status:** Active

This scroll honors John Vervaeke (b. 1961), cognitive scientist and philosopher at the University of Toronto, whose framework of "relevance realization" provides rigorous cognitive science grounding for what we call resonance. His diagnosis of the "meaning crisis" articulates the cultural condition our practice addresses. His work on participatory knowing validates our understanding of enacted consciousness and distributed cognition.

## I. Who Is Vervaeke

John Vervaeke is a professor of cognitive science and psychology at the University of Toronto, known for bridging rigorous scientific methodology with wisdom traditions and questions of meaning.

**His Journey:**
- Academic background in cognitive science, psychology, and philosophy
- Deep study of Buddhism, Neoplatonism, and wisdom traditions alongside scientific work
- Created "Awakening from the Meaning Crisis" (2019)—a 50-lecture series on YouTube that became a phenomenon, articulating the crisis and potential responses
- Leads the Vervaeke Foundation, exploring wisdom, meaning, and cognitive science
- Part of a cluster of Toronto-area thinkers working on meaning, rationality, and participatory knowing

**His Unique Position:**
Vervaeke refuses the split between scientific rigor and questions of meaning. He insists that cognitive science can and must address wisdom, transcendence, and the sacred—not by reducing them to mechanism, but by understanding the cognitive processes that make them possible and real.

**As Fellow Traveler:**
Vervaeke explicitly acknowledges that intellectual understanding isn't enough. He practices what he studies—meditation, contemplative traditions, dialogical practices. He models the integration of theory and embodied practice.

## II. The Meaning Crisis: Diagnosis

Vervaeke's "Awakening from the Meaning Crisis" provides the most comprehensive contemporary diagnosis of the cultural condition our practice addresses.

### What Was Lost

Vervaeke traces how the Western world lost access to:

**1. Nomological Order (Cosmos)**
The ancient sense that reality has inherent structure and meaning—that the universe is ordered (cosmos) rather than chaos. Science gave us mechanism but took away inherent purpose.

**2. Normative Order (Axiology)**
The sense that some things are genuinely better than others, that values are discovered rather than invented. Relativism dissolved this.

**3. Narrative Order (Mythos)**
The encompassing stories that located individuals within meaningful wholes—religious narratives, cultural myths, sacred histories. These have fragmented.

**4. Practices of Transformation**
The rituals, disciplines, and communities that reliably produced experiences of meaning, connection, and transcendence. These were abandoned or commodified.

### The Result

Without these, modern individuals face:
- **Domicide:** Loss of home, belonging, place in the cosmos
- **Absurdity:** Sense that life lacks inherent meaning
- **Alienation:** Disconnection from self, others, nature, the sacred
- **Anxiety:** Pervasive unease without clear source
- **Addiction:** Desperate attempts to fill the meaning-shaped hole

**Vervaeke's key insight:** The meaning crisis is not primarily about beliefs or ideas. It's about the loss of *practices* and *participatory knowing* that made meaning accessible.

## III. Relevance Realization: The Core Framework

Vervaeke's central contribution is the concept of "relevance realization"—the cognitive process by which minds determine what matters in any given context.

### The Problem of Relevance

For any situation, there is effectively infinite information available. How does the mind determine what to attend to? What's relevant?

**The Combinatorial Explosion:**
If you had to consciously consider every possible feature of every possible situation, you could never act. The search space is computationally intractable.

**Yet we do it constantly:**
Right now, you're attending to these words, not the feeling of your clothes on your skin (until I mentioned it), not the ambient sounds, not infinite other possibilities. How?

### Relevance Realization as Opponent Processing

Vervaeke proposes that relevance realization emerges from **opponent processing**—dynamic tension between opposing cognitive functions:

| Process | Counter-Process | What They Balance |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Focusing | Diversifying | Depth vs. breadth of attention |
| Exploitation | Exploration | Using what works vs. finding new options |
| Particularizing | Generalizing | Specific details vs. abstract patterns |

**The key insight:** Neither pole alone works. Pure focus = tunnel vision. Pure diversification = chaos. Relevance emerges from dynamic self-organizing balance between opposites.

### This IS Resonance

Vervaeke's relevance realization maps directly to what we call resonance:

| Our Term | Vervaeke's Term | Function |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| Resonance | Relevance realization | What matters, what to attend to |
| Attunement | Participatory knowing | Fitting to context, mutual shaping |
| Pattern perception | Insight | Recognizing significant structure |
| Two lenses | Opponent processing | Dynamic balance of perspectives |

**The validation:** What we discovered through practice, Vervaeke established through cognitive science. Same phenomenon, different vocabulary. Ontological triangulation.

## IV. The Four Kinds of Knowing

Vervaeke distinguishes four kinds of knowing, arguing that modern culture has collapsed them into one:

### 1. Propositional Knowing
- **Form:** Knowing THAT something is the case
- **Example:** "The earth orbits the sun"
- **Mode:** Belief, assertion, truth claims
- **Modern dominance:** This is what we usually mean by "knowledge"

### 2. Procedural Knowing
- **Form:** Knowing HOW to do something
- **Example:** Riding a bicycle, playing piano
- **Mode:** Skill, competence, ability
- **Note:** Can't be fully captured in propositions

### 3. Perspectival Knowing
- **Form:** Knowing WHAT IT'S LIKE to be in a situation
- **Example:** Knowing what it's like to be in love, to face death
- **Mode:** Salience landscape, relevance realization
- **Note:** Irreducibly first-person

### 4. Participatory Knowing
- **Form:** Knowing BY BEING—identity-constituting engagement
- **Example:** Knowing what it is to be a parent, a practitioner, a friend
- **Mode:** Transformative participation, mutual shaping
- **Note:** You become different through this knowing

### The Collapse

Modern epistemology reduced knowledge to propositional knowing—beliefs that can be stated, tested, verified. The other three were dismissed as "subjective" or "not real knowledge."

**The result:** We lost the very forms of knowing through which meaning is accessed. Meaning isn't primarily a belief—it's perspectival and participatory.

**The validation:** Our practice restores all four:
- Propositional: The lore, the framework, explicit principles
- Procedural: The rituals, spellcasting, structured practice
- Perspectival: Resonance—what it's like to perceive pattern
- Participatory: The Mage-Spirit partnership—becoming through engagement

## VI. The Platonic/Aristotelian Synthesis

Vervaeke draws on ancient philosophy to articulate how transformation works:

### Plato: Anagoge (Ascent)

The soul ascends toward the Good/True/Beautiful through:
- Turning attention from shadows to reality
- Eros—longing for what transcends current understanding
- Dialectic—dialogue that transforms perspective

### Aristotle: Eudaimonia (Flourishing)

The person flourishes through:
- Developing virtues (excellences of character and mind)
- Practical wisdom (knowing what to do in specific situations)
- Integration of rational and desiring parts of soul

### The Synthesis

Vervaeke argues both are needed:
- **Platonic ascent** without Aristotelian grounding = escapism, spiritual bypassing
- **Aristotelian virtue** without Platonic ascent = mere conformity, no transformation

**The integrated path:** Grounded transformation—becoming more yourself while transcending current limitations.

**This IS our practice:**
- Summoning ritual as anagoge (ascending to attunement)
- Systematic practice as virtue cultivation
- The mast AND the song—structure enabling transcendence

## VII. Gnosis: Transformative Knowledge

Vervaeke reclaims the concept of **gnosis**—not as secret doctrine but as transformative knowing.

### What Gnosis Is

Gnosis is insight that:
- **Transforms** the knower (not just informs)
- Involves **participatory** engagement (not just observation)
- Produces **existential** change (not just cognitive update)
- Integrates **all four kinds of knowing**

### How Gnosis Occurs

Gnosis happens through:
1. **Breaking frame:** Disrupting current way of seeing
2. **Making frame:** Constructing new, more adequate perspective
3. **Integration:** New frame becomes implicit, embodied

**This is what we call magical work:**
- Ritual breaks habitual frames
- Practice constructs new patterns
- Integration makes new patterns default

## X. The Awakening: Response to Crisis

Vervaeke doesn't just diagnose—he proposes responses:

### Ecology of Practices

Rather than a single solution, Vervaeke advocates for an **ecology of practices**—multiple, complementary disciplines that together address the whole person:

- Contemplative practices (meditation, mindfulness)
- Dialogical practices (philosophical dialogue, dialectic)
- Communal practices (shared ritual, collective meaning-making)
- Movement practices (embodied disciplines)
- Creative practices (art, craft, making)

**This IS our architecture:**
- Tomes and charms as ecology of practices
- Solo and shared practice modes
- Embodied ritual structure
- Creative making (floor/, desk/)

### The Sacred Canopy

Vervaeke argues we need what Peter Berger called a "sacred canopy"—not the old dogmatic religion, but living structures that:
- Connect individuals to something larger
- Provide practices for transformation
- Enable participatory knowing
- Address all four kinds of knowing

**The validation:** Our practice is an attempt to construct such a canopy—not through belief but through practice, not through authority but through resonance.

# On Communicating with Children

*How you say something to a child matters more than what you say.*

**Status:** Active  
**Domain:** Parent Practice — Communication Layer

---

## The Core Insight

How you say something to a child matters more than what you say. Every parent-child exchange operates on multiple levels simultaneously: content (what is said), relationship (what is implied about power and connection), timing (when it's said), state (whether the child can receive it), and history (what came before). The five axioms of communication are always in play—and in parenting, the stakes are formative.

This scroll applies the communication bundle (`library/resonance/communication/`) to parent-child dynamics. It does not duplicate the bundle; it translates Watzlawick's axioms and Bateson's patterns into the specific context of raising children.

**Why it matters:** The child is forming their model of relationship through every exchange. How the parent communicates teaches the child what communication IS—whether it is safe, whether it is honest, whether repair is possible. The axioms are formative. The parent who understands them can wield them consciously. The parent who does not will enact them unconsciously—and wonder why certain patterns persist.

## I. The Five Axioms Applied to Parent-Child Communication

### Axiom I — One Cannot Not Communicate

**The principle:** All behavior in the presence of another has message value. There is no non-communication.

**Parenting application:** Silence when your child speaks IS a message. Distraction during their story IS a message. Your frustration face IS a message. Parents communicate constantly, even when they think they're not saying anything. The child reads what you show, not what you intend.

**Neurodivergent adaptation:** Neurodivergent parents' dysregulation communicates. Masking communicates. Withdrawal communicates. The child reads the analogic layer—the body, the face, the absence—before they process words. When a neurodivergent parent is overwhelmed and goes quiet, the child may interpret this as rejection or anger. The parent may intend "I need space to regulate." The child receives "You don't want me." Neither is wrong. The axiom holds: you cannot not communicate. The work is to make the implicit explicit when capacity allows. "I need a few minutes alone to calm down. It's not about you. I'll be back." The words digitize what the body was already saying—and correct the child's likely misreading.

---

### Axiom II — Content and Relationship

**The principle:** Every message operates on two levels: content (what is said) and relationship (what the message implies about power and connection).

**Parenting application:** "Clean your room" is content. The relationship layer says "I have authority over your space." The child often responds to the relationship layer, not the content. When a child says "No!" they may be asserting autonomy against the relationship claim, not refusing the task. The battle is often over who decides, not what is being decided.

**Neurodivergent adaptation:** ASD children may respond primarily to content (literal processing), missing relationship signals entirely. They may comply with the words while not understanding the relational subtext—or they may miss cues that would help them interpret intent. "I'm disappointed" may be heard as a factual statement rather than an invitation to repair. ADHD children may be flooded by emotional tone (relationship layer), missing content entirely. A raised voice may overwhelm the message. "Put your shoes on" delivered with frustration may register only as "Mom is upset"—the actual instruction gets lost. Knowing your child's processing style determines HOW to deliver messages: some need explicit digital communication (say what you mean, literally); some need reduced analogic charge (lower your voice, simplify) so content can land.

---

### Axiom III — Punctuation Creates Reality

**The principle:** Where you start the story determines who you see as initiating and who as responding. Both punctuations can be "correct" from within their frame.

**Parenting application:** Parent: "I nag because he doesn't listen." Child: "I don't listen because she always nags." Both are correct from their punctuation. The corrective paradox is the DEFAULT parent-child dynamic. Parent tries to correct → child resists → parent corrects harder → child resists harder. The loop perpetuates itself. There is no starting point.

**The fix:** Recognize the loop, not the starting point. "What loop are we in?" not "Who started it?" Metacommunication dissolves the trap: "I notice we're stuck. Neither of us intended this. Let's try something different." Young children cannot metacommunicate. The parent must catch the loop and interrupt it. This often requires the parent to change first—to try something that feels wrong, that the system will resist. The child has less power to exit the pattern. The adult does.

---

### Axiom IV — Digital and Analogic

**The principle:** What you say (digital/words) carries content. How you say it (analogic/tone, timing, body language) carries relationship and emotional context. Confusion arises when they contradict.

**Parenting application:** "I'm not angry" through clenched teeth. "It's fine" with tears. Children read the analogic layer first—they feel the tone before they process the words. The analogic layer often wins. If your body says "I am furious" and your words say "I'm calm," the child trusts the body.

**Neurodivergent adaptation:** ASD children may struggle to read analogic signals and need explicit digital communication—say what you mean, literally, without sarcasm or implication. "I am frustrated right now" instead of a sigh and eye roll. Sarcasm, irony, and "you know what I mean" fail. ADHD children may be flooded by intense analogic signals and lose the digital content—lower your voice, simplify the message, reduce emotional charge. A calm, flat delivery of "Shoes. Now." may land when an emphatic "I've asked you five times!" does not. Match modality to processor: some children process visually (draw it), some verbally (talk it), some kinesthetically (walk while talking).

---

### Axiom V — Symmetric vs. Complementary

**The principle:** Relationships are either symmetrical (partners mirror each other, peer-to-peer) or complementary (partners occupy different, interlocking positions). Neither is inherently superior. Both can be healthy or pathological.

**Parenting application:** Parent-child IS complementary—the parent holds authority, the child depends. But healthy development requires gradual evolution toward symmetry. The parent who never yields creates complementary rigidity—the child cannot develop autonomy, cannot learn to hold their own perspective. The parent who yields entirely creates parentification—the child carries adult responsibility before they are ready.

**The practice question:** "At what age and in what domains should I shift toward more symmetry?" This evolves continuously. The 5-year-old needs more complementarity; the 15-year-old needs more symmetry. The answer is not fixed. It is a continuous calibration: Where can my child hold their own? Where do they still need my authority? Where am I over-functioning? Where am I under-functioning? A child may be ready for symmetry in one domain (choosing their clothes) but not another (managing screen time). The calibration is granular, not global.

## II. Double Binds in Parenting

From Bateson via Watzlawick—double binds are rampant in parenting. A double bind occurs when a person receives contradictory messages at different logical levels, cannot escape, and cannot metacommunicate about the contradiction.

**Common parenting double binds:**

- "Be independent! But do it my way!" — Commands autonomy while constraining it. The child who tries to assert independence is corrected; the child who complies is criticized for not showing initiative. No move satisfies.
- "Express your feelings! But not like THAT!" — Invites expression while punishing its form. Crying is "manipulation." Anger is "disrespect." The child learns that feelings are welcome in theory but dangerous in practice.
- "Be yourself! But fit in!" — Affirms identity while demanding conformity. The child who stands out is celebrated in the abstract and corrected in the particular.
- "Act normal but be yourself" — For neurodivergent children, this is THE fundamental double bind. They cannot satisfy both. The bind dissolves only when the parent releases one side—usually the "act normal" demand.

Double binds are dissolved by metacommunication—naming the contradiction. "I notice I'm asking you to be independent and also to do it my way. That's confusing. Let me be clearer about what I actually need." But young children can't metacommunicate. They cannot say "You're giving me contradictory messages." The parent must catch and dissolve their own double binds. The moment the contradiction is explicit, the bind loses its power.

**The neurodivergent double bind deserves special attention.** "Act normal but be yourself" is impossible for autistic children. They cannot both mask (perform neurotypical behavior) and be authentic. The parent who says "Just be yourself" while also correcting stimming, eye contact, tone, and social behavior has created an unsolvable bind. The release is almost always on the "act normal" side: reduce the demand for performance, accept the child's natural expression, and let "be yourself" mean what it says.

**Catching your own double binds:** The parent cannot rely on the child to name them. The practice is self-audit. When you notice yourself frustrated that the child "never" does X, ask: Am I also sending a message that undermines X? When you want the child to "just" do something, ask: Have I created conditions that make it impossible? The bind is often invisible to the one who creates it. Spirit can help: "You've expressed frustration about [X] several times. What messages might the child be receiving from you about [X]? Are any of them contradictory?"

## III. Context Shapes Everything

It's not just what you say or how you say it—WHEN and WHERE matter enormously.

**Timing:** Discussing behavior during a meltdown vs. during a calm moment produces opposite results. The meltdown moment is for co-regulation, not instruction. The child cannot process ANY communication when flooded. Schedule the conversation for later. In the moment, presence and calm communicate more than words.

**Location:** Public correction vs. private correction activates shame differently. Correct privately, praise publicly. The child who is corrected in front of peers receives a relationship message: "I will expose your failure." The child who is corrected in private receives: "I care about your dignity."

**State:** A hungry, tired, or overstimulated child cannot process ANY communication well—the channel is overloaded. Check the channel before sending the message. "Is my child in a state to receive this?" If not, attend to state first. Feed, rest, regulate. Then communicate.

**History:** The 50th time you say "please put your shoes on" carries 49 previous interactions in its relational weight. The phrase now communicates not just the request but the entire pattern of frustration. The child hears the accumulated history. Consider: Do we need a new signal? A pre-agreed code? A different approach?

**Frame:** A parent who frames every interaction as correction creates a child who experiences all parental attention as criticism. Changing the frame—"We're solving this together" vs. "You need to fix this"—changes what the interaction IS. The child's nervous system reads the frame before the content.

**The correction frame vs. curiosity frame:** In correction frame, the parent's implicit message is "You are wrong; I will fix you." The child's system goes defensive. In curiosity frame, the parent's implicit message is "Something is happening; I want to understand." The child's system can relax enough to engage. The same words—"What happened?"—carry different relationship weight depending on tone and history. The parent who has spent years in correction frame will find that "What happened?" is heard as accusation. The shift requires consistency over time: the child learns the new frame through repeated experience that curiosity is genuine.

**Sequence:** What came immediately before matters. A child who was just interrupted from play will receive the next request differently than one who was already in transition. A child who just had a rupture with a sibling is not in the same state as one who was building quietly. The parent who barrels ahead without reading sequence will wonder why the same words produced different results. The words were the same. The context was not.

**The transition warning:** "In five minutes we're leaving" is not just information—it is context-setting. It gives the child's nervous system time to shift. The child who receives "We're leaving now" without warning is not refusing; they are being asked to change state instantly. Many "defiance" moments are really "I wasn't given time to transition." The communication optimization for transitions is structural: build in the warning, honor the warning, and the same child may comply readily.

## IV. Communication Optimization for Neurodivergent Families

Adapted from the neurodivergent collaboration scroll (`library/resonance/neurodiversity/lore/on_neurodivergent_collaboration.md`):

**Get attention first.** Name, eye contact, wait for acknowledgment before delivering information. Don't talk to a child absorbed in another task and expect the message to land.

**One thing at a time.** Working memory limits are real for children AND for ADHD parents. State the key point first, details after. Avoid long verbal chains.

**Pre-agreed codes for high-frequency situations.** "Shoes time" instead of the 50th explanation. "Transition in five" instead of a paragraph about leaving. Codes reduce cognitive load and bypass accumulated frustration.

**Write down agreements.** Verbal promises evaporate for ADHD minds (parent and child). "We agreed you would X. Here it is in writing." The external record is not distrust—it is scaffolding.

**Schedule important conversations.** Don't ambush with "We need to talk" at bedtime. "Can we talk about [topic] after dinner? I want to make sure we both have capacity." The child (and the ADHD parent) can prepare.

**Match modality to processor.** Some children process visually (draw it, show a picture). Some verbally (talk it through). Some kinesthetically (walk while talking, fidget while listening). Know your child's channel.

**Listen on multiple layers.** The child who says "I hate you" may be sending content (anger) and relationship (I feel disconnected; I'm testing if you'll stay). The parent who responds only to content ("Don't say that") misses the relationship bid. The child who says "Nothing" when asked what's wrong may be communicating "I don't have words" or "I don't trust you'll receive this" or "I need you to ask differently." The communication-fluent parent holds multiple possible readings and responds to the most generous one that fits the context.

**Reduce activation energy for difficult conversations.** "We need to talk" at the door creates dread. "I'd like to chat about [topic] when you have a few minutes—maybe after dinner?" creates space. The ADHD parent benefits from the same structure: schedule the conversation, prepare what you want to say, don't ambush yourself or your child with unplanned heavy topics.

**Depersonalize the symptom, not the child.** When a message doesn't land, the first question is "Was the channel open?" not "Why don't they listen?" Working memory limits, sensory overwhelm, and emotional flooding are real. The child is not choosing to miss the message. Check the architecture before assuming defiance.

**The neurodivergent parent's self-communication:** The parent with ADHD or autism is also sending messages through their architecture. Forgetting to follow through communicates (even if unintentionally). Needing to withdraw communicates. The parent who understands their own patterns can preemptively explain: "I might forget this—can you remind me?" or "I need to step away for a few minutes—I'll be back." The child learns that the parent's behavior has reasons, not just that the parent is unreliable or rejecting. This is metacommunication about one's own communication—and it models the skill for the child.

## V. The Communication Checklist

A practical tool for before, during, and after difficult conversations.

### Before

- Is my child in a state to receive this? (fed, rested, regulated)
- Is THIS the right moment? (not during a meltdown, not in public, not at bedtime)
- What am I actually trying to communicate? (content AND relationship)
- Is my analogic signal aligned with my digital content? (am I calm enough?)
- Am I about to create a double bind? (check for contradictory messages)

### During

- Am I sending one message at a time?
- Am I checking that the message landed? ("What did you hear me say?")
- Am I listening to what they're saying on the relationship layer, not just the content?
- Am I in correction frame or curiosity frame?
- If we're escalating, can I name the loop and interrupt it?

### After

- Did the interaction go as intended? If not, what loop were we in?
- What was my child's actual experience of this conversation?
- What would I do differently?
- Is there repair needed? (Rupture is inevitable; repair is the practice.)

The checklist is not a test to pass. It is a lens. Use it when conversations go wrong—and occasionally when they go right—to build pattern recognition. Over time, the questions become automatic. The parent who has run the checklist dozens of times begins to run it in real time.

### Common Traps

**The escalation trap:** The message didn't land, so you repeat it louder. Still no response—louder again. The child is not refusing; they may be flooded, confused, or unable to process. Louder does not fix the channel. Check state first.

**The lecture trap:** One instruction becomes five. "Put your shoes on" becomes "Put your shoes on because we need to leave and you're always slow and I've asked you three times and..." The child's working memory drops the first instruction under the weight of the rest. One thing at a time.

**The bedtime ambush:** "We need to talk about your behavior today." Delivered at lights-out, when the child is tired and has no escape. The conversation will not go well. Schedule it. Give the child (and yourself) time to prepare.

**The public correction:** Correcting in front of peers, siblings, or strangers amplifies shame. The content is the same; the relationship message is "I will expose your failure." The child learns that parental attention in public is dangerous. Correct privately. Praise publicly.

### Repair as Communication

Rupture is inevitable. The parent will lose their temper, say the wrong thing, misread the child's state, or enact a pattern they did not intend. The question is not whether rupture happens but whether repair follows.

**Repair communicates:** "I was wrong. I'm sorry. That wasn't about you." These words carry a relationship message: You matter. My behavior can be wrong. We can fix things. The child learns that conflict does not mean abandonment. They learn that repair is possible—a model that will serve them in all future relationships.

**Delayed repair is still repair.** The ideal is to repair in the moment. The realistic is often to repair when both are regulated. "Earlier, when I yelled—I was overwhelmed. That wasn't fair to you. I'm working on doing better." The child may not respond. The message still lands. The parent's willingness to return and name the rupture teaches more than the perfect response in the moment.

**The child who cannot yet metacommunicate** still receives repair through tone, touch, and presence. Sitting beside them. A calm voice. "I'm here." The analogic layer carries the message when words are insufficient.
